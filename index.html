Up Here

Up here the world has its own horizon. The light poles blast a white haze over the empty parking lot on the lower half and the sky leaves an empty starless black on the upper half. The usual-light concrete is darkened with wet glaze today, like the shiny floors of modern coffee shops. The wide ramp leans into the floor below, the tall light poles are scattered throughout, and the heavy parking blocks are set along the perimeter. The enormous size of this space gives you the freedom to race around like a speed skater on an ice-skating rink. That’s what this place is: a squared, board-skating rink.

...

Skating in the 2 am morning pulls a slow and cool breeze out of the still and suspended air. The wind blows with a pleasant yet icy sharpness, cutting out the mental-clutter. And because of the shower earlier on, there’s a hint of that concrete smell leaking in the air, giving an earthy aroma in every stride. While I’m drifting around all I hear are the wheels underneath rolling on the pavement so consistently that it’s white noise, and everything else is silent. Even my thoughts are muted, unable to come out in the first place because they’re drowned by it all, gliding out here. I’m just feeling, not thinking, pondering, worrying, imagining, or doing whatever else with my fucking head that makes me miserable. I roam around, give a kick and feel the gust that comes with it fade into a light trace as I stretch into a float. And then I give another kick.

...

Nature and city have a partnership up here. A much different relationship than what’s encompassed in urban parks for example (which I do enjoy spending time in) but with the occasional honks ripping through the leaves and over the benches, I’m always reminded that I’m in a city. Up here, nature and city tag-team: I feel like I’m sight-seeing at the downtown lights through a sophisticated high-rise office window, but on the other side of the lot I feel like I’m gazing through foreign and fresh green air of a rural hiking trail. It’s revitalizing, to roll in-between both nature and city-life perfectly balanced on both sides.

...

Usually, I look over and Trevor will already be at the other side of the lot – he’ll show off and fly around like a bullet. Josh will be over at the perimeter, carving in-between and around the car stoppers. Will and Ian like to hang around the middle, near the entrance of the ramp, trying to flip their boards and practice new tricks. It’s why we come up here, to escape the modern society of clutter and information and noise, a society that has transformed human into cyborg. Up here, we escape and just be – in the now, without the burden of thinking. Up here, we revert back from programmed machines to uncomplicated creatures that breathe, sees, and feels, free from the juggling of

“why?” or
“what for?” or
“what will I do after this?” or
“how will this improve me?” or
(the even more ridiculous in my humble opinion)
“what is my purpose?” or
“what am I supposed to be?”

Up here, your purpose is to circle from point A to point A, however you want to, without time limit, without responsibility. You glide around, you breathe, and you look at the view. The freedom is the human equivalent of dogs sticking their heads out the car window. It’s not just therapeutic, it’s natural. That’s why I’m up here – I escape and find that freedom.

10/7/19 - 6:20 am

Lately, I've been skimming books more so than reading them start to end. I'll go to Barnes & Noble, walk down some random aisle, grab a book that piques my interest, glance at the table of contents, flip to the chapter that sounds most appealing, then read a paragraph, a page, or the whole chapter. I can understand why the avid reader would hate me for this, but I think it's a great way to get into the reading groove and explore new genres. I don't know, what's your opinion on this?

Anyway, this is how I spent my afternoon yesterday. I found a book called "Becoming Supernatural" by Dr. Joe Dispenza. I admit although I was attracted to the book cover, the title kind of put me off - it sounded like all those other pseudo-self-help books that I've been trying to avoid lately (the one genre I'm trying to stay away from at the moment) but I gave it a try anyway. Flipped to the chapter about staying in the present.

It was a pleasant surprise. I'll try to give a summary of the chapter in the most logical flow and in my best recollection:

Essentially what Dr. Disepnza argues is that in order to be in the present, one cannot have a familiar past or a predictable future. By having a familiar past (recalling events, memories, etc.) then your thoughts evoke the feelings associated with the past, and those feelings evoke the thoughts associated with the past - you get into a thinking-feeling loop. Then your past becomes your future because everything is predicted and the feeling/thoughts are already assumed. Consequently, the narrative that you write in your head reaffirms those thoughts and feelings when those future events do come, thus fueling the thinking-feeling loop even more. So how does one avoid this loop? By keeping your energy internal, as external events are always in competition for our energy. By conserving our energy, we can utilize it for creative tasks and allow the unpredictable and unanticipated events to occur in our lives, which break us out of the thinking-feeling loop and ultimately allow us to stay in the present.

There's a lot more depth to the chapter, but these are the nuts and bolts. This is something most of us probably know already, but it's a good reminder to have; allow unanticipated events to break us out of the monotonous Groundhog Day limbo.


An Introvert's Party

Chattering: left, right, distant, and near. Music hovering in the sky. A ‘ping’ - glasses gently touch. A heavy mug set on the counter. A chair scoots back. Typing.

Coffee shops are like bowls of soup, different sounds mixed together to create noise. A very special kind of noise. You see, sitting over near the windows, typing away at this with my back facing the party, I’m comfortable in the chaos. I’m not awkwardly walking around expected to contribute - facing the windows I’m just sight-seeing and people-watching, car after car, pedestrian after pedestrian.

Do you see the allure? The “coffee shop noise” doesn’t point its finger at the awkward ones on the dance floor; it opens its arms and creates a quiet vacuum - the eye of the storm. And so introverts like myself flock to these squares and throw $4, not for the cup but for the seat.

It’s within etiquette to put headphones on and type away on a laptop. It’s within etiquette to read a book. It’s within etiquette to play board games. It’s not the traditional party - it’s the introvert’s party (I wonder if weed cafes will be similar though, once that becomes mainstream here).

Anyway, before you wonder why people ‘waste’ so much money on coffee, remember that they might not be paying for the coffee, but for tickets to the party.
